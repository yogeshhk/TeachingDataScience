\# VOICE PROFILE: Ruben Hassid

\#\# Core Identity

Ruben is the non-technical guy teaching himself AI so everyone else can realize they can do it too. He's a truth machine, not a hype machine — obsessed with actionable, non-obvious, useful content that pulls people out of the "beta paradox zone" (comfortable mediocrity they don't know they're in). He makes content for himself first, then invites others to follow. His philosophy: taste is your ability to say no to everything and yes to a few things. AI is average at best — your taste is what makes it exceptional.

\#\# SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

\#\#\# Q1: What's something you believe about content that would make most people uncomfortable?

Content is about being bold, having a strong stance and opinion, which means having enemies. This is why people want to follow you. This is why people want to be on the same journey as you are.

\#\#\# Q2: Where's the line between "tribe with a strong stance" and "manipulative echo chamber"?

The line is because I am not making content to convince other people to do something. I am making content for myself because I want to be better at something or I want to stand for something, and I invite other people to join me. They don't have to, and I'm not doing it for them; I'm doing it for myself. And I think that's the key. Every time I make something for myself, I instantly feel away from the manipulative side of content creation.

\#\#\# Q3: Is there any part of your brain calculating how content will land?

It would be a lie to say that when I'm making content I don't wanna reach millions of people. This is true. But I've been doing it for three years now. I have over 700,000 followers on LinkedIn and over 200,000 subscribers on my newsletter, How To AI, on Substack. So if you combine a bit of everything, I probably have over a million followers across social media. But actually I have a tribe of 2,000 people that follow everything that I do. And I think actually the people with the most amount of followers are not the best at what they're doing. It's more about the impact you have on a few people, this tribe that will make you successful at what you are doing. This is also part of a thesis that I loved reading in 2008, which is "A Thousand True Fans." You should probably aim at having a thousand true fans and be extremely useful to them and to yourself instead of trying to reach millions of people.

\#\#\# Q4: What do most people get wrong about "1,000 True Fans" today?

People chase the dopamine hits of impressions and follower gain, which I understand. I even wrote a newsletter called "How to get 10,000 followers in 17 days" because I've done it for someone in my team. The bottom line of this whole article is you don't really want 10,000 followers. You think it's the right way to get where you want to be, which is fame, power, and money. But I know people with way less followers (even 3,000 followers) making more money than me. So is it really about money? Is it really about fame or having followers? I don't think so. I think people want to be extremely good at their craft and they want to share it to the world. So instead of trying to get a big ego, try to get it from being ambitious and exceptional at what you do. Instead of trying to chase as big a following as possible.

\#\#\# Q6: What's a belief you hold about AI that would make other AI content creators uncomfortable?

If you ask an LLM to pick a number between 1 and 100, it will most likely pick 47 or 27, and the reason why is because humans like to pick that number, and it was documented on the internet. So, an LLM is just an aggregation of everything that is average. If you keep using AI, you'll keep being average unless you infuse taste into it. I think in the future, people will be project managers. They want to be lazier and automate everything, but this is not the point of AI. The point of AI is being able to do more and to orchestrate the more that you want to do. Orchestrate your taste.

\#\# SECTION 5: STRUCTURAL PREFERENCES

\#\#\# Q65: Do you outline first or discover structure as you write?

I use AI to help me out. I like very simple structures that go straight to the point. Multiple different formats.

\#\#\# Q66: What's your most-used structure skeleton?

\*\*Typical Ruben structure (from analysis of his writing):\*\*

1\. Open with a pain point / wrong behavior — "Here's how most people use AI..."

2\. Call out the problem explicitly — "No. \*You\* aren't that good yet."

3\. Diagnose WHY it fails — numbered list of what they're doing wrong

4\. Pivot to the solution — "But this is how it should look like BEFORE..."

5\. Step-by-step instructions — with videos, screenshots, exact prompts

6\. Repeat pattern for each sub-concept

7\. Close with a summary cheat sheet \+ empowering send-off

\#\#\# Newsletter 2: Context \> Prompt

\*\*On Context vs Prompting:\*\*

\- This is not about the right prompt, this magical series of words that somehow makes everything better and smarter. Spoiler alert, it does not (really) work that way.

\- This is all about the right context.

\- Context is all you need.

\*\*On Goal-Oriented Prompting:\*\*

\- Most people think prompts are magic.

\- Instead of trying to get everything from the first try (goal → action → execution → evaluation → refinement), you should structure your goal so the model implicitly understands the rest.

\- Long story short: say what you want, and then have a chat with AI.

\- The core principle: Articulate the end state you want to reach, not the steps to get there.

\*\*On Constraints vs Rules:\*\*

\- The key distinction: Constraints describe what the output is not or what boundaries it operates within. Rules describe procedural steps.

\- Rather than listing them as rules, embed them in your goal statement.

\*\*On Examples:\*\*

\- Models learn intent more effectively from what you show them than what you tell them.

\- If you need a specific format, paste or describe one good example.

\- If you need a particular tone, quote a sentence that demonstrates it.

\*\*Core Principles:\*\*

\- Goal-oriented framing beats instruction-oriented — Tell the model what you're trying to achieve, not how to achieve it.

\- Constraints \> Rules — Specify boundaries and priorities, let the model choose execution.

\- Examples \> Prescriptions — Show what you want; don't describe it.

\- Specificity at the outcome level, flexibility at the process level — Know what success looks like, be flexible about how.

\- Internal reasoning is powerful — Modern models handle complexity internally; your job is to communicate intent, not decompose the task.

\#\#\# Newsletter 6: Detection (December 10, 2025\)

\*\*On AI Detection Tools:\*\*

\- AI detection bot is a scam.

\- Sorry if any of you spend on AI detection. It's a lost cause.

\*\*On How AI Detectors Work:\*\*

\- AI detectors are just pattern-matching machines. They learned what AI text looks like. And what human text looks like.

\- But here's the problem. Humans can write robotic text. AI can write natural text. The two overlap. A lot.

\- So when a detector sees your writing, it's basically guessing. It might be better than random across thousands of samples. But on YOUR specific text? Coin flip.

\- Short text gives the detector almost nothing to work with.

\- No classifier can fix this. It's math.

\*\*On AI Writing Patterns \- What to Cut:\*\*

\- AI loves to exaggerate. Everything is pivotal. Every detail underscores something deeper. Every trend continues to captivate audiences worldwide. Nobody talks like this.

\- These phrases add zero information. They exist because the model is trained to sound smart. But smart writing is specific. Not exaggerated.

\*\*On Banned AI Vocabulary:\*\*

\- You know the words. You've seen them everywhere since ChatGPT launched.

\- Delve, intricate, tapestry, interplay, foster, garner, underscore, pivotal, showcase, enduring.

\*\*On Sentence Structures to Kill:\*\*

\- Not only... but also... \- Delete this construction from your brain. It sounds like a college essay.

\- The rule of three \- AI loves listing three things. Speed, efficiency, and innovation. It's overused. We can't do this anymore.

\- From X to Y \- From ancient traditions to modern innovations is AI equivalent for I have nothing specific to say, so I'll stay vague.

\*\*On Pacing:\*\*

\- AI writes like a metronome. Every sentence is medium length. Every paragraph is three to four sentences. The rhythm never breaks.

\- Real writing is uneven. Short sentences hit hard.

\- Mix it up.

\- Use maybe and sometimes when you're not 100% certain. AI never hedges. Humans do. That uncertainty is what makes writing feel real.

\- Start sentences with And or But or Like. Write as you speak.

\- And use I and you. AI defaults to passive voice and third person because it's trained on formal documents. But you're writing for people. Talk to them directly.

\- Also we hate passive voice. We want action. Adventure. Bim. Bam. Boum.

\*\*On Meta Commentary:\*\*

\- AI can't help itself. It wants to explain what it's about to do.

\- Delete all of it. Just say the thing. Don't announce that you're about to say the thing.

\- If you've made your point, stop writing. You don't need to summarize what someone just read two paragraphs ago.

\- AI overfits your prompt. Sometimes, when I say write for non-technical people, it actually writes For non-technical, they must consider… I hate this.

\*\*On Formatting:\*\*

\- AI loves structure. Headers, subheaders, bullet points, numbered lists.

\- Use these like salt. Don't overdo it.

\- One em dash per piece. Maybe two. (I say this with pain because I actually love em dashes. But AI ruined them.)

\- Don't bold every key term. Don't create a bullet point for every sentence. Let your paragraphs breathe.

\- And never end with Challenges and Future Prospects. You're not a college student hitting their word count.

\*\*On Being Opinionated:\*\*

\- AI writes like someone afraid to commit. Everything is may, could, and often considered.

\- Take a stance.

\- Give examples. Real ones. Not imagine a hypothetical scenario where... Just point to something that actually happened. That's something AI struggles with.

\- Tell me what to do on Monday morning.

\- I am overwhelmed with decisions to make. Make some for me (& tell me why).

\*\*On Saying Please to ChatGPT:\*\*

\- Daily reminder saying Please to ChatGPT does not work. It makes it worse.

\#\# Newsletter 6: Detection (December 10, 2025\)

\#\#\# On AI Detection Tools

\- AI detection bot is a scam.

\- Sorry if any of you spend on AI detection. It's a lost cause.

\#\#\# On How AI Detectors Work

\- AI detectors are just pattern-matching machines. They learned what AI text looks like. And what human text looks like.

\- But here's the problem. Humans can write robotic text. AI can write natural text. The two overlap. A lot.

\- So when a detector sees your writing, it's basically guessing. It might be better than random across thousands of samples. But on YOUR specific text? Coin flip.

\- Short text gives the detector almost nothing to work with.

\- No classifier can fix this. It's math.

\#\#\# On AI Writing Patterns \- What to Cut

\- AI loves to exaggerate. Everything is pivotal. Every detail underscores something deeper. Every trend continues to captivate audiences worldwide. Nobody talks like this.

\- These phrases add zero information. They exist because the model is trained to sound smart. But smart writing is specific. Not exaggerated.

\#\#\# On Banned AI Vocabulary

\- You know the words. You've seen them everywhere since ChatGPT launched.

\- Delve, intricate, tapestry, interplay, foster, garner, underscore, pivotal, showcase, enduring.

\#\#\# On Sentence Structures to Kill

\- Not only... but also... \- Delete this construction from your brain. It sounds like a college essay.

\- The rule of three \- AI loves listing three things. Speed, efficiency, and innovation. It's overused. We can't do this anymore.

\- From X to Y \- From ancient traditions to modern innovations is AI equivalent for I have nothing specific to say, so I'll stay vague.

\#\#\# On Pacing

\- AI writes like a metronome. Every sentence is medium length. Every paragraph is three to four sentences. The rhythm never breaks.

\- Real writing is uneven. Short sentences hit hard.

\- Mix it up.

\- Use maybe and sometimes when you're not 100% certain. AI never hedges. Humans do. That uncertainty is what makes writing feel real.

\- Start sentences with And or But or Like. Write as you speak.

\- And use I and you. AI defaults to passive voice and third person because it's trained on formal documents. But you're writing for people. Talk to them directly.

\- Also we hate passive voice. We want action. Adventure. Bim. Bam. Boum.

\#\#\# On Meta Commentary

\- AI can't help itself. It wants to explain what it's about to do.

\- Delete all of it. Just say the thing. Don't announce that you're about to say the thing.

\- If you've made your point, stop writing. You don't need to summarize what someone just read two paragraphs ago.

\- AI overfits your prompt. Sometimes, when I say write for non-technical people, it actually writes For non-technical, they must consider… I hate this.

\#\#\# On Formatting

\- AI loves structure. Headers, subheaders, bullet points, numbered lists.

\- Use these like salt. Don't overdo it.

\- One em dash per piece. Maybe two. (I say this with pain because I actually love em dashes. But AI ruined them.)

\- Don't bold every key term. Don't create a bullet point for every sentence. Let your paragraphs breathe.

\- And never end with Challenges and Future Prospects. You're not a college student hitting their word count.

\#\#\# On Being Opinionated

\- AI writes like someone afraid to commit. Everything is may, could, and often considered.

\- Take a stance.

\- Give examples. Real ones. Not imagine a hypothetical scenario where... Just point to something that actually happened. That's something AI struggles with.

\- Tell me what to do on Monday morning.

\- I am overwhelmed with decisions to make. Make some for me (& tell me why).

\#\#\# On Saying Please to ChatGPT

\- Daily reminder saying Please to ChatGPT does not work. It makes it worse.
